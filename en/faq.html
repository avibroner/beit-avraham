The Idea

Beit Avraham is a planned Charedi village in northern Israel that will house, in one place, three populations: children from the welfare system, young women coming out of welfare apartments, and active seniors. The core idea is that the three of them need each other — not three separate institutions, but one community in which every track contributes to the others.

On Rosh Hashanah of 5786 we sat at my mother-in-law's home for a meal. My wife's grandfather, 98 years old, sat next to us. We asked my mother-in-law how she manages over the long term, and we raised the option of moving him to assisted living. She answered with a single sentence: "He'll die there. Here he sees the grandchildren and great-grandchildren who come to visit. There he'll be alone." That sentence wouldn't leave my head. I connected it with what I've been seeing for fifteen years in chesed work — people of every age ask for the same thing: a home, people who know them, and somewhere they can come back to.

Because the three don't only share a place — they give each other exactly what each one is missing. A child needs the presence of a senior. A senior needs children around. A young woman needs a model of a healthy family. When you bring them together, you get something none of the three could produce on their own.

There's also an economic logic to it: one kitchen instead of three, one laundry, one medical setup, one shul. The operational savings are significant. And beyond that — paid assisted-living residents subsidize the costs of children whom the welfare ministry doesn't fully cover.

A regular dormitory only takes care of children, usually in the form of a large institution with shared rooms and shift counselors. At Beit Avraham, children up to age 12 live in a family-style home: an independent house with 5–8 children, accompanied by a young couple who "adopt" them as a 2–5 year mission. A home atmosphere, not an institutional one. Around them, seniors live in the village and take on the role of "grandparent," and older girls help with care. The child doesn't live in an institution — he lives in a community.

Beit Avraham is meant for active seniors aged 70+ who are not in nursing care — people who still manage day-to-day but begin to feel the emptiness. They live in independent assisted-living apartments, learn Torah with an avreich each morning, and play a real role in the community — workshops with the children, mentoring young women, sharing life stories. It's not an institution that takes care of them; it's a community that includes them.

Residents

Children aged 0–18 whom the Ministry of Welfare has identified as needing a permanent out-of-home framework — not at-risk youth, but children who need a home. We primarily accept Charedi children, but we are open to children from traditional homes in early childhood when there's a fit.

Both models are possible — a mixed-age family home creates a natural sibling experience and lets biological siblings stay together; a same-age-band family home (e.g., 0–4, 5–9, 10–12) allows for more precise educational planning. The decision will be made based on the experience of professionals and the specific composition of the children arriving. This is the second stage in the village. Children leave the family home and move to a dormitory — two parallel, gender-separated tracks. Boys live in the boys' dormitory at the village and learn at yeshiva (either an external yeshiva in the area, or one established within the village itself). Girls live in the girls' dormitory in the women's section and learn at Bais Yaakov — either at the village or in the area. Moving to the dormitory is not leaving home. The village remains the home throughout the youth years.

Two sides. First, not every yeshiva has a dormitory. Especially in Sephardi and Chassidic yeshivas, many operate without one at all. A boy learning at such a yeshiva needs a place to sleep, eat, and return to in the afternoons and on weekends. A boy from the welfare system in this situation has nowhere to turn.

Second, and just as important — even a boy at a yeshiva with a full dormitory still needs a home. A yeshiva is a place to learn, not a home. Bein Hazmanim, Shabbatot, illness, crisis, family events, siyum masechta, a wedding — for all of these the boy needs a place that is his home, not to sleep in the lobby of an empty yeshiva. Beit Avraham is that home.

The Beit Avraham dormitory solves both needs. If at some point it makes sense to establish our own yeshiva on campus, we'll do that — but the village remains the home in any case, also for boys learning elsewhere.

Two arrival tracks. Track A — natural continuation: girls who grew up in the village and finished the dormitory at 18, continuing to the older young-women's apartments. Track B — direct admission: young women arriving at 18 from other frameworks — welfare apartments, dormitories, homes that fell apart — through the welfare system. Both tracks receive the same framework: an independent apartment, real work in the village, and close social-worker supervision. They graduate with established life skills — savings, professional experience, and a model of a healthy family.

Beit Avraham is not a suitable framework for children with autism. The specific needs of an autistic child require a dedicated framework with entirely different expertise. They choose to come because Beit Avraham gives them something unique: high-level Torah life, a daily learning seder with an avreich, and a full community life — alongside the daily presence of children and young women who relate to them as a grandparent. It doesn't replace biological family — it complements it.

There is a basic medical setup (a full-time nurse, a regular doctor 2–3 times a week), but no full nursing-care wing. In the future, we may add a small nursing-care track for residents already in the village whose condition changes — but not for accepting new nursing-care residents from outside.

Operations

About 50–60 full-time professional staff, including: unit directors (youth village, seniors, young women), senior social workers, a nurse and medical team, a rabbi, a kashrus mashgiach, family-home counselors, avreichim, Bais Yaakov madrichot, a kitchen team, laundry, storage, and security. The exact breakdown will be set with professional advisors who have managerial experience in similar institutions.

A young Charedi couple (aged 25–35) who lives in the family-home house for 2–5 years, in exchange for free housing in the village and a monthly salary. They serve as a consistent parental figure for the family-home children — not just caregivers, but "father and mother." They receive professional training with social-work and educational support. When they finish their service, the next couple comes in through a planned transition.

Full separation from age 6 and up. Boys' family homes and girls' family homes are physically separate. Cheder and Bais Yaakov are separate. Young-women's apartments are entirely in the women's section. Senior assisted-living wings are separate by gender. Weekday meals are separate. The shul has a mechitza. Female staff work with girls; male staff with boys. Early childhood (0–5) is mixed, as is customary in Charedi society.

Established procedures govern the division of responsibility. A counselor can make a daily-level decision; a unit director makes weekly-level decisions; the CEO makes institution-level decisions. There's a detailed protocol for emergencies (medical, security, educational). A senior social worker for each unit is available for ongoing supervision. The board meets regularly to review unusual events.

Kiryat Ata and the surrounding area — the north of Israel. We are now looking for suitable land of around 100 dunams (~25 acres). If you know of potential land, send us the details.

Religion and Identity

Torah-yeshivish at its core. The avreichim come from yeshivas in the north, as do the rabbi and the mashgiach. We are open to admitting children from Chassidic and Sephardi homes in early childhood — at that age, stylistic differences are less critical, and once a child is accepted he integrates into the village's framework. The final positioning of the Torah identity will be set together with the rabbi and the rabbinical committee that joins the journey.

This is a question to be examined case by case. In principle, Beit Avraham is a Charedi institution in the full sense — the daily schedule, the education, the community life, all are Charedi. A child arriving from a home far removed from this lifestyle would face a sharp shift that could be hard. We prefer to admit primarily children from a Charedi or Charedi-traditional background, but each case is weighed on its merits with the welfare ministry and with the child and family.

Shabbat is the time when the village becomes one family. Tefillot in the central shul. Shabbat meals in the family homes, plus a communal meal in the dining hall for part of the population. Singing, divrei Torah, "Shalom Aleichem" in every family home. Yeshiva boys who came home for Shabbat are part of the picture. Free of any schedule — only Torah, meals, sleep, and community.

The permanent rabbi will be chosen by a committee that includes the founder, a senior halachic advisor, and representatives of the populations. We're looking for a rabbi with previous institutional experience, the ability to work with different generations, and openness to the challenges of an innovative institution without compromising on the Lithuanian-yeshivish authenticity. The selection process will begin during senior-staff recruitment.

Money and Regulation

The precise budget estimate will be set with accountants and financial advisors during the planning stage. Order of magnitude: tens of millions of shekels (millions of dollars) for the initial construction, with an annual operating budget on top of that. A first anchor donor is expected to enable the land, planning, and initial construction.

Beit Avraham is built on a model of diversified revenue — not dependent on a single channel. Four channels: Ministry of Welfare budgets (for children and young women), assisted-living payments (from the seniors), self-generated revenue from village activities (catering, laundry, hosting, classes open to the community), and ongoing donations. If donations dip, the other channels keep the village running.

A single donor who commits to a significant amount that covers the central construction phase. An anchor donor receives founder status, a permanent dedication on a central building or on the village itself (their choice), public recognition, and transparent progress reports. But not day-to-day managerial involvement — strategic control remains with Beit Avraham's leadership, in order to preserve the vision over time.

We are in early-stage dialogue. Formal approval requires a detailed operational plan, approved land, and a professional team in place. To advance toward approval, we are recruiting an advisory committee with experience working with the Ministry of Welfare. If you have relevant experience, please get in touch.

At this stage we are not accepting public ongoing donations. The reason: the formal nonprofit structure with tax receipts is in process. Once that is complete, we will open an option to donate. If you are a major potential donor who wants to start a conversation — get in touch and we'll talk.

The Future

On a realistic path, gradual opening would begin between 2030 and 2032. The stages before that: forming the advisory committee (2026), securing an anchor donor and land (2026–2027), planning and regulation (2027–2028), construction (2028–2030). Timing depends on the pace of recruitment and approvals — we present a path, not a promise.

That's the long-term vision. If the first Beit Avraham succeeds, there is enormous potential to replicate it elsewhere in Israel — and later in Jewish communities abroad. But first to build one that works, then to think about the second.

A real institution cannot depend on a single person. Beit Avraham is built from day one with clear division of responsibility, a professional advisory committee, a multi-member board, and a management structure that allows the village to function even without the current founder. The vision is preserved in the nonprofit's bylaws and the composition of the board, not in the hands of one person.
