What We're Here to Solve

In Israel today, three systems lead to the same outcome: isolation.

  • A senior home isolates the elderly from family.
  • A dormitory isolates the child from surrounding generations.
  • A welfare apartment isolates the young woman from a family model.

We're not here to fix three institutions. We're here to remove the separation between them.

How It Works

Three populations, four life stages. One home.
Each gives the others what they're missing.

Not three institutions side by side — one living system. A senior brings time and experience, a young woman takes responsibility, a child brings joy, and an avreich holds the spiritual rhythm. Each track exists because the others do.

Stage One · Children 0–12

They receive a home. They give joy.

They come from the welfare system. They live in a family-style home of five to eight children, accompanied by a young couple who "adopt" them as a mission. A home atmosphere, cheder or Bais Yaakov school, after-school activities, and learning with an avreich.

Stage Two · Youth 13–18

They receive structure. They give brotherhood.

A boys' dormitory and a girls' dormitory. Boys learn in yeshiva, girls in Bais Yaakov. The village stays the home — Shabbatot, breaks, and personal life events.

Stage Three · Young Women 18–22

They receive life skills. They give responsibility.

Independent apartments inside the village. Some continue from the dormitory; some arrive from outside at 18. They work in the village and prepare for family life under the guidance of a social worker. Many return after marriage as young couples.

Stage Four · Seniors

They receive meaning. They give presence.

They come from the community into paid assisted living. They continue a Torah life, with a morning learning seder with avreichim, and they accompany the children and young women around them. An active, full community.

Where It Started

A Rosh Hashanah Story

Last Rosh Hashanah, we were sitting at my in-laws' for dinner. My wife's grandfather, 98, was sitting next to us. He's lived with them for years. We asked my mother-in-law how she was doing with him long-term, and we raised the possibility of moving him to a nursing home.

"He'll die there. Here he sees the grandchildren and great-grandchildren who come to visit. There he'll be alone."

That sentence wouldn't leave my head. I thought of relatives of mine who grew up in welfare apartments. Of the children I've met for fifteen years in chesed work. Of the people I treat in MDA. People of every age ask for the same thing: a home, people who know them, and somewhere they can come back to.

So why not build all of that in one place.

What Makes This Different

Why not just another dormitory.
Why not just another senior home.

01

A first in Israel

Every dormitory and every senior home in Israel is built for a single population. Beit Avraham is the first to make a multigenerational community its core model.

02

Mutual chesed, not one-directional

A senior who pays for assisted living subsidizes the child whom the welfare ministry doesn't fully cover. A young woman caring for a child learns about herself. A child sitting with a senior gives him a reason to wake up in the morning. A circle, not a one-sided act of charity.

03

Authentically Charedi

A Charedi institution in every sense: a permanent shul, a rabbi, a kashrus mashgiach, avreichim learning with the seniors in the morning and with the children in the afternoon, full gender separation from age six and up. A Torah-yeshivish character, open to children from all streams in the early years.

04

A synthesis of proven models

We're not building another dormitory or another senior home. We're synthesizing intergenerational models that have already proven themselves — Hogeweyk (Netherlands), Humanitas (Rotterdam), Bridge Meadows (Oregon) — and building the world's first implementation that brings three populations together under a Charedi-Israeli identity.
