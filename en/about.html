About

The story behind Beit Avraham

Beit Avraham is more than a vision document. It is the intersection of a personal story, fifteen years of chesed work, and the recognition of a real gap in Israeli society. Here you'll find the people, the insight, and the road.

The Founder

Avi Broner

My name is Avraham. Not by chance. Avraham Avinu built himself on the pillar of chesed, and although I didn't choose this name, it described my path long before I understood it.

For fifteen years I've been volunteering in chesed organizations. Not from the office — from the field. Bikur Cholim, accompanying families through hard times, helping the elderly, distributing food. For four years I've been an active MDA paramedic, going out on calls even in the middle of a workday. I've learned to see people in moments of crisis, and I've learned that what holds them together is not medicine and not money — it's a steady human presence.

In parallel, I built FutureFlow — a company that develops software systems for social and business organizations across Israel. Real clients, products that work day-to-day, a team I've grown over years. That's proof I know how to take an idea and turn it into a living thing. Beit Avraham is the next step — taking all that experience and channeling it into one thing larger than us.

"If Hashem put the idea in my head, that's a sign He wants me to do something with it."

The Moment

How Beit Avraham was born

Last Rosh Hashanah, we were sitting at my in-laws' for dinner. My wife's grandfather, 98, was sitting next to us. He's lived with them for years. We asked my mother-in-law how she was doing with him long-term, and we raised the possibility of moving him to a nursing home. She answered with a single sentence: "He'll die there. Here he sees the grandchildren and great-grandchildren who come to visit. There he'll be alone."

That sentence wouldn't leave my head. I thought of relatives of mine who grew up in welfare apartments. Of the children I've met for fifteen years in chesed work. Of the people I treat in MDA, who end their lives without family at their side. People of every age ask for the same thing: a home, people who know them, and somewhere they can come back to.

And then it connected. If a senior home as we know it cuts the elder off from family, and a dormitory cuts a child off from home, and a welfare apartment cuts a young woman off from a family model — maybe the answer is not to fix the three institutions, but to abolish the separation between them. To build one place where no one is alone.

That was the moment. The rest is just work.

Not a One-Person Project

Beit Avraham is built as a team

An institution that affects three vulnerable populations cannot be built on a single founder's gut feeling. Beit Avraham is built from day one with clear division of responsibility, a professional advisory committee, and a management structure that allows the village to function even when the founder is not in the room.

Executive leadership

Avi Broner — Founder and CEO. Responsible for vision, strategy, and business development.

In role since 2026.

Professional staff at the village

Unit directors (youth village, seniors, young women), senior social workers, medical team, rabbi, kashrus mashgiach, family-home counselors, avreichim and madrichot. In total, 50–60 full-time staff.

Recruitment will begin at the establishment phase.

Advisory committee

3–7 senior advisors in welfare, regulation, architecture, philanthropy, and rabbinics. Their role: professional review, opening doors, and maintaining the professional standard over time.

Actively recruiting. → Join

Board of directors

Top governing body of the nonprofit. Responsible for approving the annual budget, strategic decisions, and oversight of transparency and good governance. Includes the founder, public figures, and donor representatives.

Will be formed during the anchor-donor recruitment phase.

The Road Ahead

Five stages from today to the village's opening

Stage 1

Foundation and consolidation

Launching the website. Recruiting the first advisory committee. Conversations with directors of experienced institutions in Israel. Formal nonprofit registration. Building professional documentation.

Active · 2026

Stage 2

Anchor donor and land acquisition

Meetings with potential donors in Israel and abroad. Securing the first anchor donor. Locating suitable land in the north of the country (Kiryat Ata and surroundings). Initial feasibility check with the Ministry of Welfare.

Expected 2026–2027

Stage 3

Architectural planning and regulation

Working with an architecture firm experienced in welfare institutions. Regulatory approvals from the ministries of Welfare, Education, and Health. Securing full construction funding. Preparing a multi-year work plan.

Expected 2027–2028

Stage 4

Construction

Phased construction, beginning with the family homes and the central shul, followed by assisted living and service buildings. Recruiting senior team leads who will lead operations.

Expected 2028–2030

Stage 5

Opening and pilot

Gradual opening: starting with a small group of children in the family homes, young women, and the first seniors. Reaching full scale within 18–24 months of operation.

Expected 2030–2032

Timelines depend on the pace of anchor-donor recruitment and on regulatory approvals. They represent a realistic path, not a promise.
