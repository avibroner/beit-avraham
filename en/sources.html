Research and Sources

The research foundation of Beit Avraham

Beit Avraham was not built on gut feeling. Every architectural decision, every life track, and every operational choice rests on two sources: fifteen years of field experience in chesed organizations, and academic research and proven models from around the world. Here you'll find the studies and models that shaped the vision, with direct links for further reading.

Intergenerational models worldwide

Generations of Hope · Hope Meadows

Rantoul, Illinois · founded in 1994 by Dr. Brenda Krause Eheart

A planned community combining foster care for at-risk children with subsidized housing for seniors

The closest model to Beit Avraham. A planned neighborhood of 60 families in which foster families have adopted hard-to-place children, alongside 50 apartments for seniors at reduced rent in exchange for 6–8 hours of weekly volunteering as "grandparents." Follow-up studies showed a significantly higher success rate than the average in the U.S. foster system, and a meaningful drop in depression among the senior residents. The model was replicated at Bridge Meadows (Portland) and at the Treehouse Foundation (Massachusetts).

Organization website →

For a deeper read: "Generations of Hope: A Story for the Children of America" by Brenda Eheart (2007).

Humanitas Deventer

Deventer, Netherlands · founded 2012

A "students in a senior home" model — free housing in exchange for company

Six university students live for free in apartments inside the senior home. In exchange, they devote 30 hours a month to social activities with the senior residents — watching sports together, technology lessons, coffee outings, or simply sitting and talking. Studies showed reduced loneliness among residents and improved quality of life. The model has been replicated in Cleveland, Lyon, Helsinki, and other cities.

Organization website (Dutch) →

English coverage: a 2017 BBC documentary; a TheMarker feature, January 2018.

The Intergenerational School (TIS)

Cleveland, Ohio · founded in 2000 by Drs. Peter and Cathy Whitehouse

A public charter school where seniors teach children

A network of public charter schools in which seniors integrate into the classroom as teachers, mentors, and volunteers. Children learn in multi-age groups, and the volunteers contribute from every stage of their lives. Longitudinal studies have published dramatic results: at-risk children close academic gaps faster, and the volunteers show meaningful improvement in cognitive function and reduced signs of dementia.

Organization website →

Key paper: Whitehouse, P. & Whitehouse, C. (2014) "Intergenerative Strategy for Sustainability" — Generations Journal.

Hogeweyk Dementia Village

Weesp, Netherlands · founded 2009

A village designed for life with dementia

A senior home designed as a small village with streets, shops, a community center, gardens, and a café. Residents experience life in a "regular village," with the medical support system kept invisible. It is not a multigenerational model, but it is a powerful architectural proof that a living environment can be designed for a sensitive population — a key insight for the planning of Beit Avraham.

Organization website →

Bridge Meadows · Treehouse Foundation

Portland, Oregon (2011) · Easthampton, Massachusetts (2006)

Successful replications of the Hope Meadows model

Two case studies of independent replication of the Hope Meadows model elsewhere in the U.S., adapted to local communities. Both have been operating for more than a decade and provide proof that the model can be replicated under different conditions — an important insight for Beit Avraham, which can serve as a proof of concept for additional Beit Avraham villages.

Loneliness and social isolation in older adults

Social relationships and their effect on life expectancy

Holt-Lunstad J., Smith T.B., Layton J.B. · 2010

PLoS Medicine, 7(7): e1000316 · Open Access

A meta-analysis of 148 studies covering 308,849 participants. The finding: people with strong social relationships have a 50% higher likelihood of surviving a given follow-up period compared to people with weak social relationships. The effect is comparable in magnitude to smoking 15 cigarettes a day, and significantly larger than the effect of obesity. Intergenerational ties were found to be especially valuable.

Loneliness and social isolation as risk factors for mortality

Holt-Lunstad J., Smith T.B., Baker M., Harris T., Stephenson D. · 2015

Perspectives on Psychological Science, 10(2), 227–237

A follow-up meta-analysis to the 2010 paper, focused specifically on loneliness and social isolation as factors distinct from social ties. The finding: social isolation increases mortality risk by 29%, subjective loneliness by 26%, and living alone by 32%. In all three factors, the effect is especially large in people under 65 — explaining why intergenerational connection is a real treatment.

U.S. Surgeon General's report on the loneliness epidemic

"Our Epidemic of Loneliness and Isolation"

An official report from the U.S. Surgeon General declaring loneliness a public health crisis. The report explicitly calls for investment in intergenerational communities, and points to models such as Hope Meadows and Humanitas Deventer as proven solutions. American donors are familiar with this report and ask specifically: "Which dimensions of this strategy are you implementing?"

Welfare children, foster care, and attachment

Adverse Childhood Experiences (the ACE Study)

Felitti V.J., Anda R.F., Nordenberg D., et al. · 1998

American Journal of Preventive Medicine, 14(4), 245–258

A joint study of the CDC and Kaiser Permanente of 17,000 adults. A strong correlation was found between the number of adverse childhood experiences (abuse, neglect, dysfunctional household) and the risk in adulthood of mental-health problems, addiction, heart disease, and shortened life. Children with 4+ adverse experiences are at 4–12 times higher risk of health problems. A powerful protective factor identified: a stable, loving adult figure in their lives — not necessarily a biological parent.

Foster-care alumni — Casey Family Programs study

Pecora P.J., Kessler R.C., O'Brien K., et al. · 2005

Northwest Foster Care Alumni Study · Casey Family Programs

A longitudinal study of 1,087 alumni of the U.S. foster-care system, ages 20–33. Only 20% completed high school on time (vs. 87% in the general population). Only 3% completed a degree. Unemployment, addiction, and homelessness rates were 4–7 times higher. The study identifies two decisive factors in success: stability in a single framework over time, and a continuous connection with an adult figure who is not paid for caring. Both are foundational pillars of Beit Avraham.

Attachment Theory and its neurological updates

Bowlby J. (1969) · Ainsworth M. (1978) · Perry B.D. (2000s–today)

A theoretical foundation for treating welfare children

Attachment theory (Bowlby and Ainsworth) holds that a child needs a primary caregiver who is stable and consistent in order to develop secure attachment — a psychological foundation for every relationship in his life. Dr. Bruce Perry ("The Boy Who Was Raised as a Dog," 2006) showed that a child's brain develops through repeated emotional interaction. The insight: a child who passes through 5–7 foster families in childhood cannot develop secure attachment. The Beit Avraham family-home model, with a fixed young couple for 2–5 years and continuous adult presence in the village, addresses this directly.

The value of intergenerational life

Generations United · State of Grandfamilies Reports

Generations United · annual reports since 2008

A U.S. organization that consolidates research and policy on intergenerational ties

An organization that summarizes the latest research each year on multi-generational integration. Their reports provide data that American donors cite: children who grow up with a stable grandparent figure show a 14–35% improvement in academic scores, 28% fewer behavioral problems, and a higher rate of stable relationships in adulthood.

Organization website →

AARP report on intergenerational integration

AARP Foundation · 2018, 2022

"The Intergenerational Connection: A National Study"

A survey of 2,500 Americans over age 50. The finding: 86% of respondents are interested in living arrangements that include the presence of younger generations. 71% are willing to give up some of their personal space in exchange for a multigenerational community. The survey provides donors with a market data point — showing that recruiting paid assisted-living residents to Beit Avraham is not a theoretical problem.

The Israeli-Charedi synthesis

The exact model of Beit Avraham — three populations (welfare children + older young women + active seniors), with young couples in family homes, avreichim in a learning seder, and a Charedi-Torah identity — does not exist in the world in this form. We are synthesizing the best lessons from each of the models above, and building the first Israeli-Charedi implementation.

This is not a "we're unique" claim. It is an operational fact: the success of Beit Avraham will also be the first proof that this model works in Charedi society in Israel, and a foundation for replication and expansion to other places in Israel and across the Jewish world.
